III. The Past

===== PAGE 20 =====
The Industrial Revolution
•
Closest historical analogue to what is happening now
•
Transition period was brutal — even when long-term outcome was positive
•
Handloom weavers didn't become factory workers overnight
•
Decades of poverty, dislocation, and political unrest (Luddite uprisings)
•
"Engels' Pause": 1780–1840 — output per worker +46%, real wages +12% [1]
•
Productivity grew; workers saw almost none of it for 60 years
•
Lesson: the transition matters as much as the destination
[1] The Technology Trap — Carl Benedikt Frey
20

===== PAGE 21 =====
The Technology Trap
•
Core distinction: labor-enabling vs. labor-replacing technologies [1]
•
Labor-replacing transitions benefit owners first, workers eventually (maybe)
•
Whether workers survive depends on political power distribution
•
Those with data and proprietary infrastructure capture the gains
•
Physical and highly interpersonal jobs historically last to fall
[1] The Technology Trap — Carl Benedikt Frey
21

===== PAGE 22 =====
Who Wins: UK vs. US vs. Germany
•
Britain invented the Industrial Revolution — then lost the lead
•
By 1910: US produced nearly twice Britain's total steel output
•
Why did Britain fall behind? Three interlocking failures:
•
Elite education was anti-scientific and anti-technical
•
The incumbent trap: pioneers with first-gen tech saw no need to change
•
Mass production suited America's unique conditions, not Britain's
•
Key lesson: being first does not mean capturing the long-term gain [1]
[1] worksinprogress.co/issue/the-decline-and-fall-of-britain

===== PAGE 24 =====
Human Computers →FORTRAN
•
Before computers were machines, "computer" was a job — held mostly by women
•
WWII: ~100 women at Moore School calculated ballistics trajectories by hand
•
The ENIAC Six: six women programmed the first electronic computer [1]
•
Work was "women's work" — tedious and low-status
•
Dorothy Vaughan: saw the IBM mainframe arriving at NASA
•
Taught herself FORTRAN — became NASA's first Black supervisor
•
Sue Finley: one-week FORTRAN course → decades-long NASA career [2]
•
Her code is still flying on Voyager probes leaving the solar system
•
Lesson: those who saw the wave and retrained crossed over
[1] history.com/articles/coding-used-to-be-a-womans-job
[2] smithsonianmag.com/science-nature/history-human-computers-180972202

===== PAGE 25 =====
Britain's Computing Loss
•
Britain also led in early computing (Bletchley Park, Turing, Colossus)
•
Then actively pushed women out of computing roles in the 1950s–60s
•
Result: knowledge drain, skills shortage, fragile centralized industry
•
Britain failed to transition from mainframes to personal computers
•
The country that invented the technology lost its position twice [1]
•
Lesson: excluding people from the transition is not just unfair — it is self-defeating
[1] chicagomag.com/city-life/August-2017/Q-A-Marie-Hicks-Google-Memo

===== PAGE 26 =====
Why History Is Incomplete
•
Previous transitions replaced physical or routine cognitive labor
•
AI is now moving into creative, analytical, and judgment-based work
•
Disruption is no longer limited to one sector or skill level
•
The pace is categorically different — generational vs. years
•
The Dorothy Vaughan question for today:
•
Who sees the wave? Who is already learning FORTRAN? And who is waiting to be told?
