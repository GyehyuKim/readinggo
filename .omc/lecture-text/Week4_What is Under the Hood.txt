===== PAGE 22 =====
Part 1: What AI Is Made Of

===== PAGE 23 =====
What is a Token?
•
AI doesn't read words. It reads "tokens”
•
"unhappiness" → "un" + "happiness" (2 tokens). This is why AI sometimes misspells or 
miscounts letters.
•
English: ~1.3 tokens per word. 1,000 words ≈ 1,300 tokens.
•
Korean: ~2.5–3.5 tokens per word. The same meaning costs roughly 2× more tokens in Korean than 
English.
•
In Llama 3, 128,256 unique tokens are used (vocabulary size)
•
This includes all the languages as well.
•
https://tiktokenizer.vercel.app/ (cl100k_base)
•
Why the tokens?
27

===== PAGE 24 =====
What is Pre-training and Base Model?
•
Base Model: The model reads trillions of tokens of text and learns patterns (pre-training) by 
playing the "predict the next token" game billions of times.
•
Fluent at language; but no manner
•
Someone who has read every book in the library, but never had a conversation.
•
Scale of modern LLMs:
•
Llama 3 was trained on 15 trillion tokens.
•
That's roughly the equivalent of 11 billion pages of text.
•
https://app.hyperbolic.ai/models/llama31-8b (It’s not working temporarily.)
28

===== PAGE 25 =====
What is Fine Tuning?
•
Umbrella process of further training a pre-trained model on a specialized dataset
•
Supervised Fine Tuning: High-quality demonstration of the expected behavior
•
RLHF: humans rank multiple model outputs from best to worst. The model learns to prefer the kind of 
answer humans rated highly.
•
https://arena.ai/
•
Analogy: Medical school is pre-training; Residency is fine tuning

===== PAGE 27 =====
Part 2: How AI Behaves and 
Misbehaves

===== PAGE 28 =====
Does AI "Think"?
•
More like No. It generates text that looks like thinking, because it has seen millions of 
examples of human reasoning during training.
•
When you ask "analyze this business case," it produces text that resembles an analysis — but it 
has no understanding of whether the analysis is correct.
•
However, various emergent behaviors have been reported, so more speculation needed.
•
Also, it depends on what “thinking” is.
32

===== PAGE 29 =====
Why Does AI Hallucinate?
•
The model is optimized to produce fluent, plausible-sounding text. There is no internal "I'm not 
sure" signal.
•
It can generate fake citations, invented statistics, and nonexistent quotes — all with perfect 
confidence.
•
Rule for professionals: every specific number, quote, or citation from AI is a hypothesis, not a 
finding. Verify independently.
33

===== PAGE 30 =====
Why is Every Answer Different?
•
"Temperature" controls randomness. 
•
High temperature = creative and varied. 
•
Low temperature = predictable and repetitive.
•
Same prompt, different run = different token sampling = different answer. 
•
AI is probabilistic, not deterministic.

===== PAGE 31 =====
Part 3: Prompting as a Skill

===== PAGE 32 =====
What is “Context”
•
An analogy:
•
Training: All the education up to college.
•
Context: The memo you hand them before a meeting.
•
Context windows have grown rapidly: 4K → 128K → 200K → 1M tokens.
•
1M is roughly 10 books.
•
But longer ≠ better. The model can get lost in irrelevant context.
•
The skill is giving the right context, not the most context.
36

===== PAGE 33 =====
Why Does Role Definition Work?
•
"You are a McKinsey partner" is not a magic spell. It shifts which patterns the model draws 
from.
•
The model has seen text from consultants, students, doctors, and poets. Role definition tells it: 
sample from that region of your training.
•
Try it: "You are a first-year analyst" vs. "You are a CFO reviewing this for the board." Same 
question, dramatically different output.

===== PAGE 34 =====
Part 4: From Using AI to 
Building With AI

===== PAGE 35 =====
Why Does Breaking a Big Request into Small 
Steps Work Better?
•
Context quality > context length. One giant prompt forces the model to split attention across 
many competing sub-tasks.
•
Sequential small prompts give the model full focus on one problem at a time. This is 
decomposition — the core skill of vibe coding.
•
Mental model: you're managing a fast but literal-minded junior team member. Clear sequential 
briefs beat ambitious all-at-once requests.
39

===== PAGE 36 =====
What is an "Agent"?
•
An agent is AI that can act, not just answer — browse the web, run code, read files, call APIs.
•
This is the shift from "AI that talks" to "AI that does." Vibe coding is possible because AI can 
now write code and run it and fix errors.
•
Business implication: prototyping that required a developer for a week can now happen in an 
afternoon. This doesn't eliminate developers — it eliminates the excuse of "I can't prototype 
because I don't code."
40

===== PAGE 37 =====
What is AI Not Good At?
•
Novel reasoning with no precedent in training data
•
Reliable math on large numbers
•
Knowing what it doesn't know
•
Anything requiring real-world verification (live data, current facts)
•
Maintaining perfect consistency across very long outputs
•
The hardest part: you often can't tell when it fails, because the failure looks just like success.
•
Closing question: "If AI gets this wrong, would I notice — and what would it cost?"

===== PAGE 39 =====
Premortem
•
https://hbr.org/2007/09/performing-a-project-premortem
•
Steps
•
Prepare the team by informing them that the project has failed spectacularly and is now in the past.
•
Give everyone two minutes of silence to generate a list of every possible reason for that failure.
•
Conduct a 'round-robin' where each member shares one reason until all unique concerns are recorded.
•
Collectively narrow the list down to the top 3–5 most critical or likely threats.
•
Dedicate the remaining time to developing actionable 'pre-measures' for these specific issues.
43

===== PAGE 40 =====
Premortem - Lessons
•
You can use it in a very small scale (e.g., right before the presentation) to spot the last minute 
problems.
•
You need to come up with a story that makes the final destructive situation quite visible and 
believable.
•
You need to clearly deliver that the good news is that you still have a time to fix it.
•
If you can come up with actionable solutions and the team handles the issues in advance, the 
team can get the self-esteem boost.
•
In contrast, if you keep eliciting the problems but not solving them, the same issue stays.
•
Sharing each person's concerns and allowing them to speak up comfortably is the key benefit.
•
Allowing each member to think independently is important.
