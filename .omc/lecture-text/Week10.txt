===== PAGE 18 =====
Practical Path 2 — Tiny Experiments
17
•
Don't try to design the perfect life. Run small experiments and let your life teach you what you actually want. [1]
•
The protocol:
•
Pick a curiosity, not a goal
•
Run it as a fixed-duration experiment ("for 14 days, I'll write 200 words every morning")
•
At the end, decide: persist, pivot, or pause — based on data, not feelings
•
Why it works: lowers the cost of starting; kills the perfectionism that prevents action
•
Loops back to Build it — you don't know your path until you walk a few steps
[1] Anne-Laure Le Cunff, *Tiny Experiments: How to Live Freely in a Goal-Obsessed World* (Avery, 2025).

Practical Path 3 - whytree
18
•
You used whytree for HW7 (web version was launched on 5/6) [1][2]
•
The three primitives map directly to today's three approaches:
•
Why Up 
Find it — each "why?" drills down to surface what was already there. You don't invent the 
answer; you uncover it.
•
How Down 
Build it — each "how?" branches out, enumerates alternatives, constructs paths. Tiny 
experiments fit here.
•
Choose it 
the weight inside both moves — every Why-Up answer demands "yes, this is the deeper 
truth — this is mine." Every How-Down branch demands "yes, I commit to this one."
•
The alternation between Up and Down is the engine: discover → build → commit → discover deeper → build 
new alternatives → commit again.
•
whytree forces all three moves at once — even when you didn't realize you were making them. That's why HW7 
surfaced the roots, the actions, and the reframings it did.
[1] https://github.com/yijisoo/whytree [2] https://whytree.io

===== PAGE 26 =====
Lesson 1 — The root underneath the goal (~42명, three flavors)
24
•
The visible answer to "what is the ultimate goal of your life?" is rarely the actual root. After 3-4 why-ups, 
students landed on one of three roots: recognition, autonomy, or family/presence.
•
Recognition (~17명): "I want to feel that my existence matters." Career complaint = recognition complaint in 
disguise.
•
Composite: I thought I wanted to switch jobs. Three whys in, it was: I want to be the person other people 
come to, and I'm not.
•
Autonomy (~11명): not validation, control. Same outward complaint, different root.
•
Composite: I told myself I wanted to start a company. What I actually wanted was to make decisions without 
asking permission.
•
Family / presence (~14명): family named literally as the answer to Q1, not as a "balance" qualifier.
•
Composite: My ultimate goal is not to miss the small moments — the laundry, the bedtime story, the laugh 
at dinner.
•
The diagnostic that matters:
•
recognition-seekers want better mirrors
•
autonomy-seekers want fewer constraints
•
family-seekers want presence.
•
They look identical from outside ("I want to leave my job") but require different actions.

===== PAGE 27 =====
Lesson 2 — The action shrinks once the root is named (~22명)
25
•
Goal: "live a life of meaning."
•
Action: eat dinner with one specific friend this week.
•
Goal: "build a brand that proves taste and commercial success aren't opposed."
•
Action: scroll Olive Young reviews tonight, write down what bothers you.
•
Goal: "be more present at home."
•
Action: go home and help with laundry.
•
Several students wrote that they almost dismissed their own action as too small to count.
•
Any thought?
•
That dismissal is the lesson. The failure mode of ambition is non-action.
•
The smallest reversible step is the one that actually moves.

26

Why-Up surfaced things you didn't already know:

===== PAGE 29 =====
Lesson 3b —For some, nothing new came up. Still OK (~10명)
27
•
Same tool, different result: not new content, but new clarity
•
Composite: Most of what came up, I already knew. But seeing it in plain words made it impossible to keep 
avoiding.
•
The medium has independent value — you can know something and still not be able to act on it because you 
can't say it
•
Discovery and articulation are different jobs. Why Tree did the second for these students. "I already knew this"
isn't failure — it's the data that articulation worked.
•
Only 2 of 54 treated "nothing new" as failure. The right test isn't "did I learn something new?" — it's "can I now 
do something I couldn't do before?"

===== PAGE 30 =====
Lesson 4 — The last question != The first question
28
•
In your Q3 answers (biggest learning), almost no one's learning matched their Q1 (ultimate goal).
•
Students who arrived with "career success" reported learnings about completion-anxiety, fear of being unseen, 
needing rest — none of which were career.
•
This is the single most teachable point about Why-Up:
•
”처음시작한질문은진짜질문이아니다.”
•
Practical implication: don't trust the first frame. Whether you're using whytree on yourself or designing a 
product for users, the framing they hand you is probably not the real one.

===== PAGE 33 =====
The big claim
31
•
If one person with AI can do what a team of ten did before, the bottleneck is no longer execution — it is 
knowing which company should be yours to start.
•
Your why is your moat. In a world where AI commoditizes execution, the only durable advantage is doing 
something only you would do.

===== PAGE 34 =====
Power and Prediction — the thesis in one slide
32
•
AI is a prediction machine. The cost of prediction is collapsing.
•
When the cost of an input collapses, the value of its complements rises.
•
Cheap electricity → expensive electric appliances
•
Cheap prediction → expensive judgment about what to predict, data to predict from, and action based on 
the prediction
•
For solopreneurs: AI handles the prediction-heavy work (writing, summarizing, generating, classifying). What's 
left is judgment, data access, and action — and those are personal, not technical.
[1] Agrawal, Gans, Goldfarb. *Power and Prediction: The Disruptive Economics of Artificial Intelligence* (2022). Ch 1-5.

===== PAGE 35 =====
Transaction cost economics — why firms get smaller
33
•
Coase (1937): firms exist because coordinating inside a firm is cheaper than buying every transaction in a 
market.
•
When AI lowers the cost of internal coordination AND the cost of buying services in the market, the boundary of 
the firm shrinks.
•
Solopreneurs are the limit case: a one-person firm that buys every other function in the market (or generates it 
with AI).
•
This is not a prediction of the future — it's already happening. Many seven- and eight-figure businesses now run 
with 1-3 people.

===== PAGE 36 =====
Why your whytree work is the prerequisite, not the addition
34
•
Without a why, the solopreneur becomes the cheapest replaceable laborer in their own one-person company.
•
The economics work, but you spent your life solving the wrong problem efficiently.
•
With a why, the solopreneur picks a problem only they would pick, serves users only they have access to, and 
builds with judgment that doesn't generalize.
•
The honest counterpoint: not everything can be a solopreneur business. Capital-intensive, regulated, network-
effect, or trust-heavy domains still need teams. Pick accordingly.
